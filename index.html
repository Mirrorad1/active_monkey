The big idea

One principle, three lenses.

The whole project rides on a single bet from theoretical neuroscience: a mind is just a thing that tries to not be surprised. Everything — perception, learning, wanting, acting — falls out of minimizing free energy. We borrow three thinkers to keep it honest.

Friston · the math

It's all free energy.

Acting vs. asking, exploring vs. exploiting — every choice is one trade-off: reduce surprise now, or seek information to reduce it later.

Seth · perception

A controlled hallucination.

What the agent "sees" is its own best guess, constantly tested against the evidence. Confidence is interoceptive — a feeling it can act on.

Bach · architecture

The world model is the self.

Its persistent model of the world only ever grows and sharpens. Its preferences are its motivational core. Keep the self coherent; let it climb.

The takeaways

Six things the climb taught us.

01

Depth, not capacity.

Adding more internal states never helped the agent learn word order. Adding one more character of memory flipped the switch instantly. The lever was always context depth.

02

Free energy is the feeling.

Low surprise is understanding. The agent's only reward is its own prediction success — no external score, no labels. "Getting it" feels good from the inside.

03

Embodiment breaks symmetry.

A bare stream of symbols can't bootstrap a concept — the math literally has no gradient to start. A body that acts in a correlated world supplies the missing push.

04

Nothing crystallizes from nothing.

Every win came from a foothold: grounding, a body, or continuous lived experience. Pure self-organization from a symmetric start always stalled — exactly as the theory predicts.

05

Wanting needs no reward.

Valence grounds in the agent's own free energy. An arbitrary cue becomes "good" just by co-occurring with low surprise — then it acts to seek that state. Desire, with nothing injected.

06

A want needs a gradient.

Knowing where you are and wanting a goal isn't enough to move toward it. Only planning over the learned map — far enough ahead to feel the goal — turns a wish into purposeful action.
